The Body Under Siege

Tracking the Path from COVID-19 Diagnosis to Recovery or Death

Day by day, the body showed two very different patterns: one led to recovery, and one led to death. This dashboard showed what COVID-19 looked like inside the body across 8,820 patients.

What does COVID-19 actually look like from inside the body?

Most COVID-19 data visualizations focus on who died, such as older people, patients with other health problems, or people from certain areas. This dashboard looked instead at how patients got worse. What changed inside the body? Which warning signs separated the people who recovered from the people who did not?

Using clinical records from 8,820 COVID-19 patients in Massachusetts from January to April 2020, we tracked how the disease progressed over time, from diagnosis to either recovery or death.

Tab 1: Vital Trajectories

From the start of diagnosis, patients began to follow two different paths. We tracked four key signals over 21 days: body temperature, heart rate, respiratory rate, and lymphocyte count. By around day 10, the difference became clear. Survivors started to improve. Non-survivors did not. Their fevers lasted longer, their heart rates stayed higher, their breathing remained more strained, and their immune cell counts were already lower early on. A vertical line is used on the graphs as a visual marker of the median day of death, day 12.

Tab 2: Complication Cascade

COVID-19 followed a clear pattern as it became more severe. Early symptoms like cough and fever could progress to pneumonia and low oxygen levels, then to respiratory failure, sepsis, ARDS, and septic shock. The important point was that some complications, like cough and fever, were common and survivable, while others strongly signaled danger. For instance, sepsis appeared in 97% of deaths but only 4% of survivors. ARDS appeared in 63% of deaths and rarely in survivors. A histogram of days to death showed that most deaths happened in the second week, between days 7 and 14.

Tab 3: Disease Severity

Zooming out from the individual body to the population level, the third tab shows that COVID-19 is a bifurcated disease. 78% of patients managed it entirely as outpatients with near-zero mortality. But once the body could no longer manage the disease at home and went to emergency or inpatient care, mortality jumped sharply. The disease does not exist on a spectrum; there is a cliff. This dashboard showed where that change happened by comparing how many patients were treated in each care setting and the death rate in each one.

The divergence begins on day one

When vital signs were tracked day by day, survivors and non-survivors followed different paths from the very start. By around day 10, survivors showed clear signs of recovery, while non-survivors continued to worsen. The gap only grew over time.

A predictable descent from one complication at a time

COVID-19 often worsened in a step-by-step sequence: fever and cough led to pneumonia, then respiratory failure, sepsis, ARDS, shock, and death. Most patients who died moved through these stages in roughly that order.

COVID-19 was mild for most, but far more dangerous once hospitalization was needed

Most patients experienced COVID-19 as an outpatient illness with near-zero mortality. In this dataset, never needed hospital care. But for patients sick enough to be hospitalized, outcomes changed dramatically: died. The disease followed two very different paths, remaining manageable for most but becoming much more lethal once it crossed the threshold into hospital-level care.
